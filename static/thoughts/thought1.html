EFFICIENT DESIGN WORKFLOW FROM A PRODUCT MANAGER PERSPECTIVE

Motivation

Product or feature design is a continuous process that involves many iterations and feedback loops, which makes it a large factor in product development cycle. By streamlining design workflow, we can significantly increase development speed and decrease friction in a team.

Breaking down a design workflow

A design workflow consists of the following items:
1. Initial design and testing
2. development
3. Iterations
We mostly will be looking at how to improve the process at stages 2 and 3.

Stage 2 - Development

After you have approved a visual design of a product, it is ready to be delivered to developers, and this is where the party starts. At this point, it is critical to make sure that developers and a designer are in complete synch and developers have everything they need to get their job done. Let's dive deeper at what we can do to make sure it all happens correctly.

In general developers need four things:
1. Interactive prototype that includes all screens and states
2. Pixel perfect visual mockup that includes all screens and states
3 Information architecture
4. Style guide or pattern library

The better the quality of the materials that designer provides the faster and easier the development process will be. Let's take a look at some things that a designer should do to make developers' life easier.

Practices that a designer should follow to save time on development side

Sometimes simple things make a huge difference. Here is my list of practices that a designer should follow to make collaboration efficient and avoid ambiguity.

Naming convention

No receipt fits all projects, that's why it is largely up to a designer to decide what kind of naming convention to use. The general idea is that such a convention is going to help everybody who interfaces with designs to do so efficiently. A designer should figure out how to adequately classify objects in a design and then distinguish names of objects that belong to different classes visually. For example, in one of my projects I put "[]" before every element that was related to "Event object." and started a name of every button with a capital letter. A designer should also avoid ambiguous object naming. For example, there should be no "ellipses", "rectangles", "group1s", etc. All these things have a purpose and should be named accordingly e.g. "like-button-background", "image-blue-overlay", "navbar-top", etc.

Avoid unnecessary group nesting

Sometimes it seems like wrapping objects in multiple groups makes a project more ordered and helps to make a design easy to manipulate, and to some extent, it is true. However, a designer should avoid overdoing it. He should only add groups when they are necessary. Nobody likes clicking on an element ten times to see what color is the background.

Perfect alignment

Have you ever experienced a developer aligning an element in a way that he thinks it should be aligned? If yes then it probably happened either because a designer had not aligned it perfectly and a developer did not understand how an element should be aligned, or a designer didnâ€™t align an element perfectly, and a developer used this as an excuse not to care. By perfectly aligning elements a designer makes it very unambiguous how they are intended to be aligned and push a responsibility on a developer to follow your guidelines.

Do not include irrelevant materials

When a designer delivers a design, he must always make a clear distinction between a production version and Ideas. The production version should only include approved designs and nothing else. It doesn't mean that a designer should never share his ideas, he should just do it separately from production materials.

Use content that is as close as possible to real content that will be there

It is very tempting to use professionally shot images, structured text data and complete user profiles in your design. I, however, strongly recommend not to do so, because if a designer does so, the way a design will end up looking in practice may be dramatically different from what a designer intended it to look like. On the contrary, if a designer knows what kind of flaws a content may have and acknowledges that, he can build his design around that content to make it look better.

Define how a design will scale

It may be obvious to a designer, not so much to everyone else, especially for people who have no design background. A designer should always define how each element will scale, including fonts, buttons, sections, etc.

Stage3 - Iterations

As your product/feature grows it is likely to change, that means you will have to change your design as well. In this section, I will describe a workflow that I find to be particularly efficient for continuous change and delivery of design. Please note that if you do not have a designer who is continuously working with you, this may not be for you.

Wha do we want to accomplish by streamlining the workflow

  • Make it easy for a designer to share the deliverables

  • Make it easy for Product Managers to review the deliverables and provide feedback.

  • Reduce the number of services we use, and documents produced thereby reducing management overhead.

Apps we will use

Invision
Craft plugin by Invision
Slack

High-level solution

I recommend using Invision as a hub, where all the stakeholders can meet and collaborate. A designer can easily upload his designs to Invision using Craft plugin, and it gets as easy as pushing one button if he is using Photoshop or Sketch. On the other hand, developers have everything they need to understand what is required of them, at their fingertips. Product Managers will enjoy powerful comment functionality, equipped with notifications, delivered via Slack and Email, as well as Scrum-inspired workflow management solution.

The workflow

1. Change or a new feature is requested
2. Designer uploads visual mockups to Invision and quickly creates an interactive prototype from them right on the spot.
3. Product Manager receives a notification about a new upload via email or Slack, checks the mockups and the prototype, leaves comments and change requests right on the prototype.
4. A designer gets notified about the comments, reviews them and makes the change, uploads a changed design. Step 3 and 4 repeat in a loop until the new design is accepted.
5. A designer uploads pixel perfect visual mockups with all the states and creates a detailed prototype. A designer should also equip the prototype with comments that provide additional information (alignment, scaling rules, etc.). PM sends a notification to developers.
6. Developers start working on implementing the new design. They review mockup and a prototype, comment and ask questions right inside the Invision. Developers also can use inspect mode to get all the alignment and color information as well as assets right from the prototype.


In my experience, using such a workflow makes things much more organic and intuitive than when using email, which increases overall efficiency and team satisfaction
