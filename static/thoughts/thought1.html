DESIGN INTEGRATION PIPELINE

Disclaimer

Sometimes simple things make a huge difference. Here is my list of things that you as a designer should do before even starting to implement any other steps.

Naming convention

No receipt fits all projects, that's why it is largely up to you to decide what kind of naming convention to use. The general idea is that such a convention is going to help everybody who interfaces with your designs to do so efficiently. You should figure out how to adequately classify objects in your design and then find a way to distinguish names of objects in different classes visually. For example, in one of my projects I put "[]" before every element that was related to "Event object." and started every name of a button with a capital letter. Also, it is a good idea ( and, if you ask me, a must have) to avoid ambiguous object naming. For example, there should be no ellipses, rectangles, group1s, etc. All these things have a purpose and should be named accordingly e.g. like-button-background, image-blue-overlay, navbar-top, etc.

Avoid unnecessary group nesting

Sometimes it seems like wrapping objects in multiple groups makes a project more ordered and helps to make design easy to change, and to some extent, it is true. However, avoid overdoing g it, only add groups when they are really necessary. Nobody likes clicking on an element ten times to just get to it.

Perfect alignment

Have you ever experienced a developer aligning an element in the way he/she thinks it should be aligned? If yes then it probably happened either because you have not aligned it perfectly and thereby didn't make it clear how an element should be aligned, or you didn’t align an element perfectly, and a developer used this as an excuse to not care. By perfectly aligning elements you make it very unambiguous how they are intended to be aligned and push a responsibility on a developer to follow your guidelines.

Do not include irrelevant materials

When you share a design with a developer, only share a Production version, leave your experiments/ideas aside to avoid confusion. Now, it doesn't mean that you shouldn't share any ideas with developers, you should, just do it separately from production design and make it clear that this is only ideas. Once again, the working design must be very clear and only contain a production version.

Use content that is as close as possible to real content that will be there

It is very tempting to use professionally shot images, structured text data, completely filled profiles in your design. However, I strongly recommend not to do so, because if you do, the way your design ends up looking in practice may be dramatically different from what you intended it to look like. On the contrary, if you know what kind of flaws your content may have, you can take certain steps to make it look better in your design.

Integration

Now when we are done with basics let’s take a look at how to create the most frictionless design delivery and integration process possible.

Goals for integration

Make communication between a developer and a designer clear, efficient, and painless. Make design delivery easy and efficient.

High-level solution

We are going to use Invision’s Inspect and Slack plugin, combined with practices outlined above to streamline communication between a designer and a stakeholder. To make design delivery as easy as one step we are going to use synchronization functionality that Invision’s Craft plugin for Sketch and Photoshop provides.

High-level solution

Apps that we will use

  • Invision

  • Craft plugin by Invision

  • Sketch or Photoshop

  • Flinto

  • Slack

Streamlining communication

Communication between a designer and a developer stands to accomplish the following:

  • Designer shares designs with stakeholders

  • Designer shares designs with stakeholders

  • Designer shares designs with stakeholders

Invision can help us facilitate and streamline all of the above.

With Invision, sharing designs is incredibly easy. A designer can upload his/her designs into Invision with a one button push if he/she has Craft plugin (works for Photoshop and Sketch). A Product manager and a developer can view, inspect and provide feedback on a design in Invision app. Essentially invision acts as a hub where all the stakeholders meet, interact and can get information that they need.

A PM can explore a design screen by screen or can use an interactive prototype that a designer can build right inside the Invision app. If a PM wants a designer to change something or ask for a team’s opinion on something he/she can do so using comments that can be attached to a particular design element to avoid ambiguity. Comments can be synchronized with Slack and Email to make sure that everybody is receiving them. You can also address comments to specific users to attract their attention.

A developer can get all the product assets and explore element alignment using “Inspect screen”. While interactive prototype, equipped with comments from a designer will leave the UX design as unambiguous as possible. Theoretically, this two things ( interactive prototype and Inspect screen) is all a developer needs to successfully implement a design.

A designer can request and collect feedback from stakeholders using comments and attract their attention when needed by moving the designs through the workflow (in progress -> needs review ), which will send a Slack message to them.
